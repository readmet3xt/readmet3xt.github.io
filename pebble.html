Case Study: Pebble

Pebble: Nurturing Workplace Happiness in the Age of Remote Work

A Royal College of Art & VISA collaboration on how we designed a virtual companion to combat stress and foster connection for a thriving workforce.

Service Design Employee Wellbeing Remote Work Virtual Companion VISA Collaboration Human-Centered Design

Introduction

The way we work has fundamentally changed. While remote and hybrid models offer unprecedented flexibility, they’ve also cast a long shadow, revealing a workforce increasingly grappling with stress, isolation, and the challenging blur between professional and personal lives. As a project lead and designer on a collaborative initiative between the Royal College of Art and VISA, I, Amaan Khan, along with my colleagues Jing Qian and Zhiyuan Zheng, saw an urgent need. How could we move beyond reactive measures and proactively design for genuine happiness and connection in this new, often distant, working world?

This case study details the journey of Pebble, a virtual companion designed to do just that. We’ll explore how we leveraged a human-centered design process — from in-depth research and collaborative workshops to iterative prototyping and user testing — to create a service aimed at boosting employee wellbeing and productivity. Our central mission: to cultivate a thriving, happy, and genuinely connected workforce, regardless of physical location.

The Challenge & Context

Our core challenge was born from a stark observation: the silent but pervasive decline in employee wellbeing. The “spark” for this project was the overwhelming data emerging, particularly during and after the pandemic, that painted a clear picture of a workforce under strain.

Our initial research, including surveys of over 1200 individuals and specific insights from VISA Innovation Centre employees, highlighted several critical issues:

  • The Emotional Toll: Employees reported significant feelings of Stress, Isolation, Distraction, and being Overwhelmed. The lack of casual, in-person interactions and the intensity of remote work were taking a heavy toll.
  • Erosion of Boundaries: The lines between work and personal life had become dangerously blurred, leading to longer working hours and a constant sense of being “on.”
  • Plummeting Happiness: The statistics were undeniable. 43% of employees rated their mental health between ‘very bad’ and ‘fair,’ and the number of those feeling ‘completely happy’ at work had nosedived from 26% to just 13% since February 2020.

But this wasn’t just about feelings; it had a tangible business impact. We found compelling evidence that:

  • Happier employees are up to 12% more productive.
  • They demonstrate 3 times more creativity.
  • Crucially, engaged and happy employees can enhance business profitability by up to 147%.

This context defined our brief: How might we leverage design to proactively foster happiness and wellbeing in the workplace, transforming it from a reactive HR concern into a core, integrated part of the employee experience? We needed a solution that understood diverse employee needs and could adapt to the evolving landscape of work.

Our Approach: Research-Driven Iteration

As the project lead, I guided our team through an intensive, research-driven design process. My responsibilities included facilitating and designing workshops, conducting research interviews, shaping the design strategy, and leading the UI design and user prototyping efforts. We believed that to create a truly impactful solution, we needed to deeply understand the nuances of employee experiences and co-create with our users and stakeholders every step of the way.

1. Understanding & Ideation (Initial Research & Workshops)

Our journey began with a broad exploration of “happiness” itself, particularly within the workplace context. We conducted initial surveys (gathering 70 responses) and then dove deep with a series of 24 workshops involving both the general public and employees from the VISA Innovation Centre (VIC). These workshops were crucial for:

  • Mapping Emotional Journeys: We used tools like “How was your week?” journey maps to understand the daily and weekly emotional ebbs and flows of employees, identifying key moments of stress and joy.
  • Uncovering Core Needs: We identified critical insights, such as the deep-seated need for regular, informal catch-ups (often lost in remote settings), the varying ways individuals regulated stress, and a strong desire for personalized wellbeing support rather than generic programs.

2. Defining Personas (Who Are We Designing For?)

The rich qualitative data from our workshops and surveys allowed us to move beyond assumptions and develop data-backed personas. We explored several archetypes based on factors like emotional susceptibility to work content, influence by others, and self-management of emotions.

A key strategic decision emerged from a user segmentation validation workshop with the VIC team and the public:

  • The “Explorer” as a Primary Focus: We discovered that the majority of individuals identified with or had characteristics of the “Explorer” persona — someone aware of their mental status, open to trying wellbeing activities, but unsure what works best or how to stick with it.
  • A Foundational Experience: Critically, we found that other user groups often transitioned through an “Explorer” phase. This meant that designing for the Explorer wasn’t just targeting the largest segment but also addressing a foundational experience relevant to a broader audience.

Our primary persona, “James,” a 28-year-old product designer new to his company and working from home, embodied the Explorer’s challenges: feeling easily frustrated, struggling with focus, and lacking informal interaction with colleagues.

3. Co-creating Pebble (Ideation Workshops & Concept Development)

With a clear understanding of our target user, we moved into co-creation ideation workshops, primarily with the VISA Innovation Centre team. The guiding question for these sessions was: HMW (How Might We) help the explorer to build a foundation of high wellbeing and better prepare for the challenges from work?

These collaborative sessions were instrumental in shaping the core concept of Pebble. We realized that a one-size-fits-all approach wouldn’t work. Instead, the idea of different “Pebble types” emerged — virtual companions with distinct personalities and interaction styles tailored to the needs of different personas (Explorer, Mentor, Feeler, Doer).

4. Prototyping & Testing (Bringing Pebble to Life)

We then translated these concepts into interactive prototypes. As the UI designer, I focused on creating an interface that was not only functional but also felt empathetic and engaging. We conducted user testing with 5 participants to gather initial feedback on the core Pebble experience.

The Solution: Pebble — A Tailored Happiness Assistant

Pebble is envisioned as a virtual assistant integrated into an employee’s workflow, promoting happiness and productivity in three key ways:

Personalized Matchmaking & Onboarding

Users discover their unique Pebble companion through a conversational, quiz-based matchmaking process. This isn’t a one-time setup; the Pebble learns and adapts through daily check-ins, building an emotional engagement over time.

Fostering Deep Work & Productivity

Pebble helps users enter a state of “flow” by offering 1-minute cognitive exercises to prepare the mind and tools to reduce distractions, directly linking focused work to a sense of accomplishment and long-lasting happiness.

Enhancing Social Connection through the Virtual Café

To combat isolation and recreate spontaneous office interactions, Pebble features a “Virtual Café.” This allows employees to easily join or create informal coffee chat rooms with colleagues they know or might want to connect with.

Building Healthy Working Habits & Boundaries

Pebble acts as a “Happiness Assistant,” gently prompting users to set work-life boundaries (e.g., reminders to take breaks or log off) and reflect on their achievements and mood.

Iteration & Key Insights from Testing

The feedback from our initial prototype testing with 5 participants was invaluable and directly shaped our understanding and future iterations:

  • Users Loved the Connection: Features enabling “focus,” “coffee catch-ups,” and seeing “different statuses” resonated strongly. This validated our core hypothesis about the need for tools that foster connection.
    Insight: Beyond task completion, employees crave a sense of belonging and shared experience, especially when physically apart. Digital tools must actively facilitate this.
  • Users Wished for Deeper Integration & Personalization: Feedback included desires for an “Emotion check at the start of the day,” the ability to “track and use data that supports after few days,” and “more control over groups.”
    Insight: Wellbeing isn’t a fleeting moment; users seek continuous support and agency. They want tools that learn, adapt, and provide tangible value over time through data and personalization.
  • Users Wondered About Platform Cohesion: Concerns were raised about “integration within the team (instead of pop-ups),” “integration with existing software,” and avoiding “too many ways to communicate.”
    Insight: Platform fatigue is real. For a wellbeing tool to succeed, it must seamlessly integrate into existing workflows rather than becoming another siloed application. This directly informed our future vision for Pebble.

While we didn’t have major design approaches that were completely discarded, these insights heavily influenced the prioritization of future enhancements, emphasizing the need for robust integration capabilities.

The Bigger Picture: Pebble’s Ecosystem and Future Vision

Pebble is designed to benefit a wide range of stakeholders within an organization:

Our future vision for Pebble includes:

  • Seamless Integrations: Making Pebble a natural part of the workday by integrating with platforms like Microsoft Teams and Slack.
  • Enhanced Features: Introducing daily mood tracking with notifications for support roles if negative trends persist, team-generated personalization for focus exercises, and enriched social experiences like icebreaker games in the Café.
  • Strategic Growth: A B2B marketing approach for company adoption, followed by onboarding employees through existing internal platforms, and retaining users through new features and intelligent conversational AI, potentially partnering with services like Headspace or Alexa.

Results and Learnings

The Pebble project resulted in a deeply researched, user-validated concept for a service with the potential to significantly enhance employee wellbeing and productivity. While this was an academic and exploratory project with VISA, it laid a strong foundation for how such a service could be realized.

My key personal learning from leading this project was: The profound impact of proactively understanding and addressing diverse employee well-being needs to foster happiness and productivity, particularly in evolving work environments like remote or hybrid models.

Other bold insights our team gained include:

  • Wellbeing is Not One-Size-Fits-All: The diversity of personas and their needs underscored that effective wellbeing solutions must be adaptable and personalizable.
  • Co-creation is Non-Negotiable: Involving users and stakeholders (like the VISA team) directly in the design process, through numerous workshops, was critical to uncovering true needs and ensuring the relevance of our solution.
  • Small Nudges Can Create Big Shifts: Gentle prompts for breaks, reflection, or connection, when timed appropriately, can significantly influence behavior and contribute to a healthier work culture.
  • The ROI of Happiness is Real: The strong link between employee happiness and key business metrics (productivity, creativity, profitability) makes investing in wellbeing a strategic imperative, not just a “nice-to-have.”

Conclusion

Pebble represents more than just a concept; it’s a testament to the power of design to address complex human challenges in the workplace. By starting with empathy, grounding our process in rigorous research and co-creation, and focusing on the nuanced needs of employees like “James the Explorer,” we developed a vision for a service that could genuinely bring more happiness and connection to the workday. As work continues to evolve, solutions like Pebble will be increasingly vital in building resilient, productive, and truly thriving teams.
